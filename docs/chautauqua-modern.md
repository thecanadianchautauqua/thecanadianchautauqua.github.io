# Chautauqua after the Fire — 1909 to Today

The [resort story](chautauqua.md) ends in flames on 27 August 1909. But the wheel of streets did not disappear; it became a neighbourhood. This document carries the history forward — through the tax-sale subdivision, the summer-cottage decades, the arrival of the Shaw Festival, and the modern community of residents' associations, creek stewards, oak-planters and heritage advocates who make Chautauqua one of the most distinctive places in Niagara-on-the-Lake today.

> **How to read this as a newcomer.** By the end you should be able to hold your own with a local: name the neighbourhood's organizations and what they do, explain why the oaks matter, know the neighbourhood's stake in Town Hall debates, and drop a fact or two that even a long-time resident might not know (see *Things that surprise even locals* at the end).

Source keys are in the [reference list](../references/references.md); dates and names are drawn from the organizations' own records and local news.

## 1. From resort to subdivision (1909–1922)

After the **Hotel Strathcona** burned, the **Niagara Syndicate** kept selling lots — mostly to people who wanted to build **summer cottages** — but the grand resort vision was over. In **1922** the **Mississauga Land Beach Company** bought the remaining land, laid out a **subdivision**, and began selling off the last lots. The company soon hit **financial trouble** and lost most of its unsold land to the **Town for unpaid taxes**; the Town then sold those lots off itself. **[GAC][EN]** Piece by piece, through the 1920s, the old assembly grounds passed into private hands.

## 2. The summer-cottage community (1920s–mid-20th century)

For decades Chautauqua was a **seasonal place**. The early cottages were **small, modest one- and one-and-a-half-storey dwellings, usually not winterized** — owners typically lived in them a **couple of weeks to a month** and **rented them to vacationers** for the rest of the spring–autumn season. **[arch-i-text][GAC]** That **century-old cottage-rental tradition** is, as a local heritage columnist puts it, part of what has left "an indelible mark" on Chautauqua's streetscapes — and it sits directly behind today's short-term-rental debates (see §8). **[arch-i-text]**

## 3. Becoming a year-round neighbourhood — and "SHAWtauqua"

Over the later 20th century the cottages were winterized, infilled and enlarged, and Chautauqua became a **year-round residential neighbourhood** — while keeping its curb-free, footpath-like streets and its canopy. A key catalyst was the **Shaw Festival** (founded 1962 by Brian Doherty). So many festival actors, designers and crew settled in the neighbourhood's affordable cottages that it earned its enduring nickname, **"SHAWtauqua."** **[NHR]** For a small neighbourhood, that means Chautauqua has quietly **housed some of Canadian theatre's best-known faces** — the celebrated Shaw ensemble of the Newton era (**Michael Ball, Sharry Flett, Jim Mezon, Jennifer Phipps, Goldie Semple** and others) — alongside its scenic designers and craftspeople. The festival's landscape ties run deep: longtime artistic director **Christopher Newton** (who led the Shaw for **23 years**, and died in **December 2021**) said the town's **"grand heritage tree canopy"** was one of the reasons he took the job — and the neighbourhood later planted a **grove of oaks in his honour.** **[NOTL Local][Shaw Festival]**

## 4. The Chautauqua Residents' Association (est. 1952)

The **Chautauqua Residents' Association (CRA)** is the neighbourhood's civic backbone — a **not-for-profit that has represented Chautauqua since 1952** (over seventy years, and so **older than the Shaw Festival** that gave the neighbourhood its nickname). It **advocates for residents and neighbourhood preservation with the Town**, and it **hosts social events** and the annual **Art & Garden Tour** (§9). Membership is a token **$10 a year** (by e-transfer). The volunteer board turns over at an autumn AGM at the NOTL Community Centre: **Brian Crow** was elected president for 2021–22 (succeeding **John Gleddie**), with treasurer **Ruth Denyer** and directors including **Weston Miller**, who now serves as **president**. The CRA curates neighbourhood resources on heritage (including the NOTL Museum's *Historic Chautauqua* materials and local historian **Daryl Rutt**'s documentation), on the lake and Ryerson Park (Swim Guide water-quality monitoring), and on the tree canopy. **[CRA][Niagara Now]**

## 5. Friends of One Mile Creek (est. 2003)

Water is the neighbourhood's western edge. **One Mile Creek** — named because its mouth reaches Lake Ontario about **one mile from the mouth of the Niagara River** — rises on Parks Canada land in **Paradise Grove**, meanders through the Old Town, and pools in **Lansdowne Pond** before its outlet at **Mississauga Beach**. It drains a modest **~5.2 km² watershed** (the NPCA counts **93 properties** touching the creek and its tributaries). Since the **1970s**, the **Epps Drain** (upstream of John Street) has diverted roughly **30%** of the headwaters east to the Niagara River to spare the Old Town from flooding — so the creek runs low in summer but still overflows in big storms. Its health became a community cause. **Friends of One Mile Creek (FOMC)** was **founded in 2003** by a group of landowners — **Michael Belfie, Gerry Beneteau, Bonnie Dawe, Ken Goodwin, Diana and Marek Laubitz, and Katleya and Klara Young-Chin** — after the CRA took on the cleanup of Lansdowne Pond. **[FOMC]** Milestones:

- **2005** — the **One Mile Creek Watershed Plan** (by Aquafor Beech & LURA Consulting), which mapped a decade of water-quality work. **[FOMC]**
- **2006–2012** — regular meetings, a **nine-part speaker series**, and repeated clean-ups (seven Earth Day events, four fall clean-ups). **2010** — a **water-monitoring program** began. **[FOMC]**
- **2013** — tenth anniversary; all stewardship measures from the 2005 study achieved. **2019** — **re-incorporated as a not-for-profit** (chair **Michael Fox**, vice-chair **Klara Young-Chin**, past-president **Gerry Beneteau**). **[FOMC]**
- **October 2020** — with the Town, Parks Canada and the **NPCA**, FOMC planted **450 native shrubs and plants** across King Street, partly to replace canopy lost to the **emerald ash borer** (the Town removed **876 ash trees**, **214** of them in the One Mile Creek watershed). **[NPCA][FOMC]**
- **Recognition** — NPCA **Conservation Achievement Awards** almost every year 2006–2019, a **NOTL Volunteer Recognition Award (2005)** and a **Niagara Region volunteer award (2010).** **[FOMC]**

## 6. The Great Chautauqua Tree Canopy & the Oaks Project (2016–2025)

Chautauqua's most beloved feature — the reason it is nicknamed a **"Cathedral of the Everyday"** — is its tree canopy, and at its heart are the **oaks**. It began, fittingly for a friendly neighbourhood, with an **over-the-fence conversation** in **2016**: two neighbours, scenic designer **Leslie Frankish** and **Holmes Hooke**, alarmed at how fast the mature trees were vanishing, discovered a shared resolve and set out to **catalogue every tree in the neighbourhood.** From that inventory they produced **two things** — the **Chautauqua Community Tree Plan** (a blueprint for homeowners and the Town on retaining the canopy) and the **Chautauqua Oaks Project** (the action arm). **[NOTL Local][Chautauqua Oaks Project]**

The survey turned up a remarkable fact: the canopy's most iconic element was **166 mature oaks, all of a similar age, that had self-seeded on the abandoned Crookston farmland just after the War of 1812** — a living link to 1812, christened **"The Great Chautauqua Tree Canopy."** But with the neighbourhood now built up, only **12 young oaks** were coming up to replace them. **[NOTL Local]**

So they started planting: the **first planting in 2018** put **80-plus trees along Wilberforce Avenue and Circle Street.** For a sustainable supply they partnered with **Niagara College's School of Horticulture** (professors **Mary Jane Clark** and Wakal and their students), **harvesting acorns from the heritage oaks** to grow their own offspring — the first crop of **"ChautauquaOaks" went in along Shakespeare Avenue in 2022.** By **October 2023** the project was complete on every street: **172 young "New Great Canopy" oaks** in the neighbourhood — welcomed into the gardens of **93 homeowners** — plus **over 500 trees** gifted across greater NOTL, including **Niagara Parks' Paradise Grove** and **"Christopher's Grove"** in Old Town. Helpers credited by name included former Lord Mayor **Betty Disero, J.B. Hopkins, Ruth Denyer, Mary Jane Clark and Shauna Dickson.** **[NOTL Local][Niagara College][notl.com]**

Frankish's own explanation of *why* the oaks feel the way they do is the kind of thing that can stop a local in their tracks: the heritage oaks, she noted, self-seeded **"in twos and threes, remarkably close together… they evoke a sociable relationship, a charming indication that our neighbourhood is a friendly place,"** and where the groves spread across property lines they create **"a unifying protective embrace — a comforting indication of community guardianship."** The new plantings were deliberately arranged to **replicate those sociable clusters.** **[NOTL Local]**

The project closed with a flourish: on **24 October 2025**, a **community-funded steel plaque — "more a sculpture than a plaque," fabricated by former Shaw craftsman Rob Brophy —** was unveiled at **Chautauqua Park** to honour the volunteers, championed by former Lord Mayor **Betty Disero.** A **"Witness Tree" program** and the celebrated **"Heart of Chautauqua Oak"** anchor the neighbourhood's identity to its trees. **[NOTL Local / Niagara Now]**

## 7. Nature & place — the lay of the land today

Chautauqua sits **between One Mile Creek, Lake Ontario and the Parks Canada lands** (the Commons and Fort Mississauga). **[CRA]** Its green and blue spaces:
- **Ryerson Park / Mississauga Beach** — the lakefront park that survives from the resort's Ryerson Park, with a **beach and views across to the Toronto skyline**; the CRA monitors swim-water quality here. **[CRA]**
- **Lansdowne Pond & One Mile Creek mouth** — the restored wetland edge (see §5).
- **Chautauqua Park** — a Town park and the site of the oaks plaque and community plantings. **[notl.com]**
- **The oak canopy** threading every spoke-street.

## 8. Heritage, development & the Town — the live debates

Chautauqua is not a museum piece; it is a neighbourhood negotiating growth:
- **Heritage protection.** Individual Chautauqua properties already appear on the Town's **Municipal Heritage Register** (e.g. addresses on Addison Avenue and around Chautauqua Park), and the Town has drafted neighbourhood **"character statements"** for Chautauqua. When the Town drafted an **expansion of its Heritage Conservation District**, however, Chautauqua was **left out of that particular report** — prompting **Councillor Sandra O'Connor** to ask why. Staff said Chautauqua *is* recognized as significant and would be handled with **other planning tools** (the Planning Act / Ontario Heritage Act) at the implementation stage. **[notl.com Heritage Register][NOTL Local]**
- **Short-term rentals.** The century-old cottage-rental tradition now collides with modern **short-term-rental (STR)** pressure. The Town regulates STRs under **By-law 2025-032 (signed 27 May 2025)**, requiring a licence; heritage voices warn that **"unhosted" whole-home rentals** could hollow out the community's year-round character. **[notl.com][arch-i-text]**
- **The Niagara Foundation.** Beyond the Town, the **Niagara Foundation** — a registered charity **founded in 1962** to safeguard NOTL's architectural, cultural and environmental heritage — is an important neighbour. In **December 2024** it acquired **"The Wilderness," 407 King Street**, a property of Indigenous, military, political and horticultural significance right on **Chautauqua's eastern edge**, ensuring its protection. **[The Niagara Foundation]**

## 9. Life in Chautauqua now

A newcomer will find a small, active community:
- **The CRA** runs neighbourhood **socials** and advocacy; **FOMC** runs **creek clean-ups and plantings**; the **Oaks Project** legacy lives on in stewardship and the Witness Tree program. The **NOTL Museum** also runs guided **neighbourhood walking tours** that take in Chautauqua. **[NOTL Local]**

### The Chautauqua Art & Garden Tour
Grown from a suggestion at the CRA's annual meeting, the **inaugural Chautauqua Art & Garden Tour** ran on **1 June 2024**, 1–4 p.m., **free** (donations welcomed) — **ten gardens and studios** opened across the neighbourhood. President **Weston Miller** framed it plainly: *"Historically, Chautauqua was a very artistic community."* The line-up shows how art and horticulture braid together here:
- **7 Wilberforce Ave — Leslie Frankish**, the scenic designer behind the Oaks Project, showing **set models and costume renderings** in a stage-designer's garden;
- **5 Wilberforce Ave — Holmes Hooke** (the Oaks Project's coordinator), with photographs, artwork, poems and songs;
- **61 Shakespeare Ave — Troy Miller**, with 30+ varieties of Japanese maple and an artist's studio;
- plus **13 Addison Ave** (40 iris varieties), **4 Circle St** (Jo-Ann Fraser), **2 Froebel Ave** (Benj Koffman's wildlife-habitat garden), and gardens at **26, 42 & 44 Shakespeare Ave** and **22 Wilberforce Ave.** **[Niagara Now]**

### Staying here
The century-old cottage-rental tradition continues in vacation cottages such as **Five Point Cottage** and **Shakespeare Cottage Retreat** on Shakespeare Avenue — blocks from Queen Street, steps from Ryerson Park — alongside the surviving original resort cottages locals still name (the *Dreamweaver*, *Five Point* and *Blue Pearl*). Nearby Old Town adds heritage B&Bs such as the **School House B&B**, the former 1859 Niagara Public School. **[niagarabb.com][NHR]**

### Neighbourhood of the Year (2026)
In its inaugural year, the Town's **Neighbourhood of the Year Award went to Chautauqua.** The Town announced on its official channels that *"the Chautauqua neighbourhood has been named the recipient of the Town's 2026 Neighbourhood of the Year Award,"* and — as winner of the contest run for **Neighbour Day (20 June 2026)** by **Lord Mayor Gary Zalepa**'s council — Chautauqua was to be honoured with a **municipality-supported community party on 11 July 2026.** **[Town of NOTL][Niagara Now]**

## Things that surprise even locals
- The neighbourhood's **iconic oaks are War-of-1812 veterans** — 166 of them **self-seeded on the abandoned Crooks farm just after 1812**, so the "old-growth" canopy and the town's defining battle share a birthday. **[NOTL Local]**
- Today's oaks are **literally the children of those trees** — grown by Niagara College from **acorns of the 166 originals**, and deliberately planted **"in twos and threes"** to copy how the originals sociably self-seeded. **[NOTL Local]**
- The whole oak-restoration effort started as an **over-the-fence chat between two neighbours** in 2016. **[NOTL Local]**
- One spoke of the original literary wheel quietly changed identity: **"Dickens Avenue" on the 1886 plan is "Dixie Avenue" today** — and **nobody seems to know why** it was renamed (the change is undocumented; the street still exists, at 3–10 Dixie Ave). *(local lore, corroborated by current addresses)*
- A Niagara Assembly director, **John N. Lake, went on to found the city of Saskatoon** (see [people.md](people.md)) — a prairie city with roots on this lakeshore.
- The **Chautauqua Residents' Association predates the Shaw Festival** — it has run since **1952**, a decade before "SHAWtauqua" got its nickname.
- One Mile Creek's **headwaters rise in Paradise Grove** — the very grove the Oaks Project later **gifted its heritage-oak offspring** to, quietly linking the neighbourhood's water and its trees.
- Since the **1970s the creek has run half-starved by design** — the **Epps Drain** siphons ~30% of its flow to the Niagara River to keep the Old Town from flooding. **[FOMC]**
- The oaks plaque at Chautauqua Park is **the work of a former Shaw Festival metal craftsman**, Rob Brophy. **[NOTL Local]**
- On Chautauqua's eastern edge sits **"The Wilderness" (407 King St)** — a property of Indigenous, military and horticultural significance that the **Niagara Foundation only bought (to protect) in December 2024.** **[The Niagara Foundation]**
