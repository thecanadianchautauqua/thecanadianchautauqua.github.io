## Indigenous era (pre-1781)
- **15th century (1400s)** — The **Neutral Nation (Attawandaron)** inhabit the lands around the future site of Fort Mississauga at Mississauga Point. **[W]**
- **Late 17th century** — **Seneca** (Haudenosaunee/Iroquois) people occupy the region. **[W]**
- **c. 1700** — **Ojibwe** peoples push the Haudenosaunee back across the Niagara River; their descendants become the distinct **Mississauga (Anishinaabe)** culture. **[St. Catharines Museum]**
- **18th century** — The **Mississauga** (Anishinaabe) people inhabit the area; the point and the later Chautauqua grounds take their name from them. **[W]**

## Founding, Loyalists & the Newark capital (1781–1798)
- **1781** — **Niagara Purchase:** Crown agent **Col. Guy Johnson** buys from the **Mississaugas of the Credit** a 4-mile-wide strip along the west bank of the Niagara River (Lake Ontario to Lake Erie) for "300 suits of clothing" — the legal basis for the town. **[MNCFN][CE: Niagara Purchase]**
- **1781** — Settlement founded as **Butlersburg**, named for **Col. John Butler**, commander of **Butler's Rangers** (Loyalists chiefly from the **Mohawk Valley, New York** and NE Pennsylvania, whose winter quarters stood on the west bank of the Niagara); a British military base and refuge for Loyalists fleeing the American Revolution. Later called **West Niagara**. **[W][Br][Butler's Rangers — Wikipedia]**
- **June 1784** — **Butler's Rangers disbanded**; ~**258 officers and their families remain** and settle the Niagara area, becoming the town's founding population. **[Butler's Rangers — Wikipedia]**
- **1792** — Renamed **Newark** by Lt-Gov. **John Graves Simcoe**; designated **first capital of Upper Canada** (today's Ontario). **[W][CE]**
- **17 September 1792** — The first Parliament of Upper Canada convenes at **Navy Hall**, Newark. **[W][PC]**
- **1793** — At Newark, Simcoe advances the **Act Against Slavery (1793)**, among the first anti-slavery legislation in the British Empire. **[CE]**
- **1792–1796** — Legislature meets at Navy Hall five sessions in total, through June 1796. **[W]**
- **1796–1799** — **Fort George** built just south of the town after Britain cedes Fort Niagara to the U.S. **[W][PC]**
- **1797** — Capital moved to **York** (Toronto); Newark too exposed on the border. **[W]**
- **1798** — Town renamed **Niagara**. **[W]**

## The Crooks family & the land (c. 1788–1811)  *(see [crooks-and-1812.md](crooks-and-1812.md))*
- **c. 1788** — **Francis Crooks** opens a mercantile house at **Fort Niagara** (US side) with Robert Hamilton; half-brothers **William** and **James Crooks** follow (1790–91). As Fort Niagara passes to the U.S., the family crosses to **Newark** and forms **W. & J. Crooks**, building the estate **Crookston** on Mississauga Point — the future Chautauqua grounds. **[DCB: Crooks]**
- **1 May 1811** — W. & J. Crooks launch the schooner **Lord Nelson** at Niagara. **[USS Scourge]**

## War of 1812 & destruction (1804–1816)
- **1804** — **Mississauga Point Lighthouse** built — the **first lighthouse on the Great Lakes**. **[W]** (Later demolished to build Fort Mississauga.)
- **9 June 1812** — **nine days before war is declared**, Lt. Melancthon Woolsey's **USS Oneida seizes the *Lord Nelson*** on suspicion of smuggling (no proof); the U.S. Navy arms her as the **USS *Scourge*.** **[USS Scourge]**
- **27 May 1813** — **Battle of Fort George**: after a two-day bombardment from Fort Niagara and the U.S. fleet, American forces capture Niagara. The estate of **James Crooks** at Crookston (the future Chautauqua grounds) is bombarded in this opening assault. **[W][NHR]**
- **8 August 1813** — the **Scourge (ex-*Lord Nelson*)** founders with the **USS *Hamilton*** in a night squall off Fourteen Mile Creek; **~84 men drown.** The two wrecks survive intact on the lakebed — a **National Historic Site (1976)**. **[USS Scourge]**
- **10 December 1813** — Retreating American forces **burn the town of Niagara** to the ground, turning out residents into a December snowstorm. **[W]**
- **19 December 1813** — British capture **Fort Niagara** in retaliation. **[W]**
- **1813–1816** — **Fort Mississauga** built at Mississauga Point from the rubble of the burned town; completed 1816. **[W]**

## Rebuilding & the 19th century (1815–1880)
- **December 1814** — the **Treaty of Ghent** ends the war and fixes the border; a ruined **James Crooks** resettles at **West Flamborough (Crooks' Hollow)**, later building **Upper Canada's first paper mill (1825–26)**. His Crookston estate lies wrecked, held by his heirs. **[DCB: Crooks]**
- **1817 → 1914/1930** — a U.S. court rules the *Lord Nelson* seizure **illegal (1817)**, but **embezzled court funds** delay payment; the **Crooks heirs win compensation only a century later** (~$23,644 to 25 descendants). **[USS Scourge]**
- **After 1813** — Town rebuilt, the residential core shifting around **Queen Street** and **King Street**, with the courthouse moved out of range of Fort Niagara's guns. **[W]**
- **1809 / rebuilt post-war** — **St. Mark's Church** (founded as a parish 1791; stone church 1809) used as a barracks and hospital during the war, later restored. **[W]**
- **1830** — **Niagara Harbour and Dock Company** incorporated; shipbuilding boom. **[W]**
- **1845** — Steamboat **London** launched, "the fastest boat on the upper lakes." **[W]**
- **1846** — *Smith's Canadian Gazetteer* records a thriving commercial town: two weekly newspapers, churches, professionals, active port. **[W]**
- **1847** — **Court House** built on Queen Street (today a Shaw Festival theatre and former Parks Canada HQ). **[W]**
- **1859** — **Niagara Public School** built — the town's first public school. **[W]**
- **c. 1880** — Post office adopts **"Niagara-on-the-Lake"** to avoid confusion with Niagara Falls, 19 km south. **[W][Br]**

## The Chautauqua era (1886–1916)
- **August 1874** — *(parent movement)* **John Heyl Vincent** and **Lewis Miller** found the first **Chautauqua Assembly** on **Lake Chautauqua near Jamestown, New York** — the model the Niagara Assembly would copy. **[Chautauqua Institution Archives]**
- **early 1880s** — **Robert Warren**, Niagara's postmaster, and Toronto associates plan a summer assembly modelled on the **Chautauqua Institution** of western New York. **[NHR][EN]**
- **12 June 1885** — Earliest known **press notice** of the project: the *Niagara County News* (NY) reports the "establishment of the Canadian Chautauqua." **[NYS Historic Newspapers]**
- **May 1886** — The Town of Niagara agrees to **exempt the ~92-acre parcel from taxation for 20 years**; the **Niagara Assembly** (formerly the *Fort George Assembly*) buys the **Crooks property** (~90–92 acres) at Mississauga Point. **[EN][NHR]** *(Sources vary 90 vs 92 acres and James vs William Crooks — see [chautauqua.md](chautauqua.md).)*
- **1886** — Printed **grounds plan** prepared (NOTL Museum record 986.088); grounds laid out on a **radial "spoke-and-wheel" plan**: ~500 cottage lots on 99-year leases, streets radiating from **Circle Street**, named for **religious reformers** (Wilberforce, Knox, Wycliffe, Wesley, Luther), **educators** (Froebel, Vincent, Ryerson), and **poets/essayists** (Longfellow, Tennyson, Addison). Build-out of a **4,000-seat amphitheatre**, the **Hotel Chautauqua** (built 1887), a **wharf**, a **rail spur from John Street** (Michigan Central Railway), and a **generating plant**. **[NHR][EN][GAC][NOTL Museum 986.088, 2000.027]**
- **1888** — **First full season opens.** Buffalo papers (12–13 July 1888) cover "The Canadian Chautauqua on the Lake, Ont." and its **inter-denominational missionary** programme. Steamers *Cibola* and *Chicora* run daily Toronto–Lewiston with Niagara stops; Michigan Central trains bring visitors mid-June to mid-September. **[EN][NHR][NYS Historic Newspapers]**
- **14 June 1890** — The season **opens 14 June and runs 12 days** (per the *Lockport Daily Journal*), the typical ~11–12-day mid-June assembly. In **July 1890** the **New York Central** advertises **excursion trains "To the Canadian Chautauqua"** from Buffalo. **[NYS Historic Newspapers]**
- **1891** — Posted rates: season pass **$1.00**, day pass **10¢**; hotel **$2.00/day to $14.00/week**. Classes in English, History, Political Science, Physical Education, Music, Art, Botany; concerts and divine services in the amphitheatre. **[EN]**
- **1894** — The **Niagara Assembly goes bankrupt** (under-leased lots, over-assessment/mortgage troubles, too few accommodations). **[NHR][NN2]**
- **after 1894** — The **Niagara Syndicate** buys the resort and renames the hotel the **Hotel Strathcona**. **[NN2]**
- **1894** — Subdivision **survey plan** of the Assembly grounds drawn (NOTL Museum record 2000.027), naming the avenues. **[NOTL Museum 2000.027]**
- **27 August 1909** — At **3:20 p.m.** the **Hotel Strathcona is destroyed by fire** — completely gone within ten minutes — reportedly started by an exploding curling lamp in the servants' basement quarters; guests flee, some leaping from balconies. The Syndicate does not rebuild. **[NOTL Museum 986.088][NN2][NHR]**

## 20th century to today (1916–present)
- **1962** — **Shaw Festival** founded by NOTL lawyer **Brian Doherty** (with director Maynard Burgess and ten unpaid actors): two Shaw plays over eight summer weekends at the **Court House Theatre**; now a major Canadian repertory theatre. **[NOTL Heritage Portal][W]**
- **1970** — **Town of Niagara** and **Township of Niagara** amalgamate; the name **"Niagara-on-the-Lake"** is made official. **[W]**
- **1955** — Hosts the **8th World Scout Jamboree**, 11,000+ Scouts from 71 countries. **[W]**
- **1986** — Old Town designated an Ontario **Heritage Conservation District**. **[W]**
- **1996** — Named **"Prettiest Town in Canada"** (Communities in Bloom). **[W]**
- **2003** — Old Town designated a **National Historic Site of Canada**. **[W][PC]**
- **2006–2021** — Population grows **14,587 (2006) → 15,400 (2011) → 17,511 (2016) → 19,088 (2021)**. **[W]**
- **Present** — Chautauqua survives as a residential neighbourhood, its curb-free radial streets shaded by a tree canopy nicknamed the **"Cathedral of the Everyday"**; nicknamed **"SHAWtauqua"** for the Shaw Festival staff who live there. **[NHR]**

## The Chautauqua community era (1909 → today)
*Full narrative in [chautauqua-modern.md](chautauqua-modern.md).*
- **1909–1922** — The **Niagara Syndicate** sells lots for summer cottages; the resort era is over. **[GAC]**
- **1922** — The **Mississauga Land Beach Company** subdivides the remaining grounds; hit by financial trouble, it forfeits most unsold land to the **Town for unpaid taxes**, which resells it. Chautauqua becomes a **summer-cottage community.** **[GAC][EN]**
- **1952** — The **Chautauqua Residents' Association** is founded — still active 70+ years later. **[CRA]**
- **1962** — The **Shaw Festival** begins nearby; over the following decades its staff settle here, earning the nickname **"SHAWtauqua."** **[NHR]**
- **2003** — **Friends of One Mile Creek** founded to restore the creek and Lansdowne Pond. **[FOMC]**
- **2005** — The **One Mile Creek Watershed Plan** guides a decade of restoration. **[FOMC]**
- **2016** — Neighbours **Leslie Frankish** and **Holmes Hooke** begin cataloguing the canopy — the start of the **Chautauqua Oaks Project**; the survey finds **166 heritage oaks self-seeded just after 1812.** **[NOTL Local]**
- **Oct 2020** — FOMC, the Town, Parks Canada and NPCA plant **450 native shrubs**; the Town removes **876 emerald-ash-borer-killed ash trees** (214 in the watershed). **[FOMC][NPCA]**
- **2021 (Dec)** — Shaw's long-time artistic director **Christopher Newton** dies; the neighbourhood plants **"Christopher's Grove"** of Chautauqua oaks in his honour. **[NOTL Local]**
- **2022** — The Town adopts the **Chautauqua Community Tree Plan.** **[notl.com]**
- **2023** — The **Chautauqua Oaks Project meets its goal** — 172 new "next-generation" oaks plus **500+ trees gifted** across NOTL. **[NOTL Local]**
- **1 June 2024** — The **inaugural Chautauqua Art & Garden Tour** (10 gardens/studios, free). **[Niagara Now]**
- **27 May 2025** — NOTL's **short-term-rental By-law 2025-032** takes effect, framing the modern debate over the neighbourhood's century-old cottage-rental tradition. **[notl.com]**
- **24 Oct 2025** — A community-funded **oak-canopy plaque/sculpture** (by former Shaw craftsman **Rob Brophy**) is unveiled at **Chautauqua Park.** **[NOTL Local]**
- **June 2026** — **Chautauqua wins the Town's inaugural "Neighbourhood of the Year" Award**; a municipality-supported party follows on **11 July 2026.** **[Town of NOTL][Niagara Now]**
