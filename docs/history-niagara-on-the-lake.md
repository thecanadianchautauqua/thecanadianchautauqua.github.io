# A Narrative History of Niagara-on-the-Lake

Niagara-on-the-Lake sits where the **Niagara River** empties into **Lake Ontario**, directly across the river from **Fort Niagara**, New York. In a little over two centuries it has been an Indigenous homeland, a Loyalist refuge, the **first capital of Upper Canada**, a battlefield, a busy 19th-century port, a summer-resort town, and finally one of Canada's best-preserved historic towns. This narrative ties the threads together; for exact dates see the [timeline](timeline.md), and for the Chautauqua neighbourhood see [chautauqua.md](chautauqua.md).

## Indigenous homelands

Long before European settlement, the mouth of the Niagara River was home to successive Indigenous peoples. The **Neutral Nation (Attawandaron)** — so named by French missionaries for staying out of the wars between the Haudenosaunee and Wendat — were the primary inhabitants of the southern Niagara Peninsula and occupied the area around the future **Mississauga Point** in the **15th century**. The **Seneca** of the **Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy** held the region through the **late 17th century**, until **Ojibwe peoples pushed the Haudenosaunee back across the Niagara River** by *c.* 1700; their descendants became the distinct **Mississauga (Anishinaabe)** culture, who lived here through the **18th century** and lent their name to **Mississauga Point** and, later, the Chautauqua resort's "Mississauga Beach." **[W][St. Catharines Museum][MNCFN]** The river was a strategic portage route around Niagara Falls, making this a contested and valuable crossroads for centuries.

The British acquired the land by treaty as the Loyalists arrived. In the **Niagara Purchase of 1781**, Crown agent **Col. Guy Johnson** obtained from the **Mississaugas of the Credit** a strip of land **four miles wide along the west bank of the Niagara River**, from Lake Ontario to Lake Erie, "in exchange for 300 suits of clothing" — the legal foundation of the town that rose at the river's mouth. Further cessions followed in the **Between the Lakes Purchase** (1784, clarified by treaty in **1792**). **[MNCFN][CE: Niagara Purchase]**

## Loyalists, Butlersburg, and the birth of a town (1781–1791)

The town owes its existence to the **American Revolution**. **Butler's Rangers**, raised in 1777 under **Col. John Butler**, were drawn chiefly from Loyalist families of the **Mohawk Valley of New York** and the northeastern **Pennsylvania** frontier, who fled imprisonment and persecution to fight for the Crown; their **winter quarters were built on the west bank of the Niagara**, opposite British-held **Fort Niagara**. The settlement that grew up around them was founded in **1781** as **Butlersburg** (also called **West Niagara**), a military base and refuge. When the Rangers were **disbanded in June 1784**, roughly **258 officers and their families chose to remain**, becoming the town's founding population — a Loyalist diaspora of English, Scots-Irish, Irish, and Palatine-German origin whose surnames still fill St. Mark's churchyard. **[W][Br][Butler's Rangers — Wikipedia]** Their muster rolls and land-grant records survive largely in **The National Archives (Kew, England)** and at **Brock University's Friends of the Loyalist Collection** — see [external-archives-source-map.md](external-archives-source-map.md).

## Newark: the first capital of Upper Canada (1792–1797)

When Britain created the province of **Upper Canada** in 1791, its first Lieutenant-Governor, **John Graves Simcoe**, chose this town — which he renamed **Newark** — as the **capital**. On **17 September 1792** the **first Parliament of Upper Canada** convened at **Navy Hall**. **[W][PC]** Simcoe's brief Newark years were consequential out of all proportion to their length: here in **1793** he pushed through the **Act Against Slavery**, one of the earliest pieces of anti-slavery legislation in the British Empire, which began the gradual abolition of slavery in the province. **[CE]**

But Newark sat **directly across the river from American guns**. Judging it indefensible, Simcoe moved the capital to **York (Toronto)** in **1797**, and in **1798** the town was renamed simply **Niagara.** **[W]** To shore up the frontier, the British built **Fort George** just south of the town between **1796 and 1799** after handing Fort Niagara to the United States under Jay's Treaty.

## The War of 1812: capture and burning (1812–1814)

The War of 1812 fell hard on Niagara. On **27 May 1813**, after a two-day bombardment from Fort Niagara and the American fleet, U.S. forces won the **Battle of Fort George** and **occupied the town for seven months.** It was during this period that **Laura Secord** made her famous **32-km walk** through American-held territory in June 1813 to warn the British of a planned attack — intelligence that contributed to the British victory at **Beaver Dams.** **[W]**

As the Americans withdrew in **December 1813**, they **burned the town of Niagara to the ground**, forcing residents out into a snowstorm — an act that prompted swift British retaliation with the **capture of Fort Niagara on 19 December 1813** and the burning of American settlements along the frontier. **[W]** From the rubble, the British raised **Fort Mississauga** at Mississauga Point (1813–1816), recycling brick from the burned town.

## Rebuilding and the 19th-century port (1815–1880)

The town rebuilt in the decades after the war, its core settling around **Queen Street** and **King Street**, deliberately out of range of Fort Niagara's cannon, with a new courthouse. **[W]** Niagara became a **thriving Lake Ontario port and shipbuilding centre**: the **Niagara Harbour and Dock Company** was incorporated in **1830**, and by the 1840s the town launched fast lake steamers (the **London**, 1845) and supported two weekly newspapers, churches, and a busy professional class, as recorded in *Smith's Canadian Gazetteer* (1846). Civic landmarks followed — the **Court House (1847)** and the town's first **public school (1859)**. **[W]**

As lake and rail tourism grew, the town's location on the lake became its brand. Around **1880** the post office adopted the name **"Niagara-on-the-Lake"** to distinguish the town from booming **Niagara Falls**, 19 km upriver. **[W][Br]** That same tourism economy made possible the **Chautauqua summer resort** (1886–1909) at Mississauga Point — see [chautauqua.md](chautauqua.md).

## The quiet century and heritage rebirth (1880s–present)

The 20th century treated Niagara-on-the-Lake gently — and that neglect proved a blessing. Bypassed by heavy industrialization, the town kept its Georgian and Victorian streetscape largely intact. In **1955** it hosted the **8th World Scout Jamboree** (11,000+ Scouts from 71 countries). In **1962** the **Shaw Festival** began staging the plays of George Bernard Shaw and his contemporaries — today a cornerstone of Canadian theatre and of the local economy. In **1970** the **Town of Niagara** and **Township of Niagara** amalgamated and the name **"Niagara-on-the-Lake"** became official. **[W]**

Recognition of the town's heritage followed: an Ontario **Heritage Conservation District (1986)**, the title **"Prettiest Town in Canada" (1996)**, and designation as a **National Historic Site of Canada (2003).** **[W][PC]** Surrounded by the vineyards of the **Niagara Peninsula wine region**, the town's population has grown steadily — from **14,587 (2006) to 19,088 (2021)** — even as the Old Town has been carefully preserved. **[W]**

Today Niagara-on-the-Lake is at once a working town, a wine-country destination, a theatre town, and an open-air museum of early Canadian history — with the **Chautauqua** neighbourhood a singular reminder of the brief, idealistic summer-assembly era.
