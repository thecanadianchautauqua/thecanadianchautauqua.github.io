# The Land Before the Wheel — the Crooks family, the war, and the road to peace (c. 1788–1886)

Before Chautauqua was a wheel of streets, it was a merchant's estate on a point of land where a river meets a lake — and where an international border was still being drawn. The story of that ground is a story of the **Crooks family**: Scottish-born traders who built a business straddling the Niagara, were torn from the American side by a hardening frontier, lost their finest ship to the U.S. Navy *before war was even declared*, watched their estate burned in that war, and then spent a **century pressing the American government for justice**. Only after all of that — seventy years after the guns fell silent — did the ruined ground find peace as a Chautauqua assembly, drawing the very Americans who had once been the enemy.

This document supplies the deep roots that predate the [Chautauqua resort](chautauqua.md); source keys are in the [reference list](../references/references.md).

## 1. A family astride the border (c. 1788–1797)

The Crookses were a Scottish merchant family from **Kilmarnock, Ayrshire**. The first to cross the Atlantic was the half-brother, **Francis Crooks (1766–1797)**, who around **1788** set up a mercantile house at **Fort Niagara** — then still a British post on the *American* side of the river, near present-day Youngstown, New York — in partnership with the powerful Niagara merchant **Robert Hamilton**. His half-brothers followed: **William** about 1790/92 and **James (1778–1860)** in **1791**. Together they ran **Crooks and Company**. **[DCB: Crooks]**

The border decided their future. As Britain prepared to hand **Fort Niagara to the United States** (agreed under **Jay's Treaty, 1794**; handed over 1796) and improved its defences in **1795**, the Crookses moved **across the river to Newark** (Niagara-on-the-Lake), on the Canadian bank. When Francis died in the **West Indies in 1797**, the surviving brothers formed the firm that would make their name: **W. & J. Crooks.** **[DCB: Crooks]**

## 2. Crookston: the estate on Mississauga Point

On the point where **One Mile Creek** meets **Lake Ontario**, James Crooks built a **brick house** and laid out an estate he called **Crookston** — the very ground the Chautauqua wheel would later occupy. (Sources place his acquisition between *c.* 1795 and an 1808 grant; the family would hold the land until **1886**.) **[GAC][EN]** From Newark and Crookston, **W. & J. Crooks** grew into one of Upper Canada's leading houses — **military provisioning, the shipment of grain and flour to Lower Canada, brewing, distilling, and potash.** **[DCB: Crooks]**

## 3. The *Lord Nelson* — seized nine days before the war

To carry their trade across the lakes, the brothers **built a schooner at Niagara**, launched **1 May 1811** and named the **Lord Nelson** — an unarmed merchant freighter. **[USS Scourge — Wikipedia]**

Then came one of the most extraordinary acts of the whole story. On **9 June 1812 — nine days *before* the United States declared war** — Lieutenant **Melancthon T. Woolsey** of the **USS Oneida** seized the *Lord Nelson* on Lake Ontario as she sailed from Prescott to Niagara, alleging she had smuggled American goods in breach of the **Embargo Act**. There was **no proof of smuggling**; James Crooks protested at once — but the coming war swallowed his protest. The U.S. Navy **armed the schooner with eight guns and renamed her the USS *Scourge*.** In a bitter irony, she sailed Lake Ontario under a flag reading **"Free Trade, and Sailors' Rights."** **[USS Scourge — Wikipedia]**

## 4. The war on the doorstep (1812–1814)

The war James Crooks had not sought now came to his door. As a **captain in the 1st Lincoln Militia**, he **commanded a flank company at the Battle of Queenston Heights on 13 October 1812**, and was **commended by Major-General Roger Hale Sheaffe.** He later set down his own account in a manuscript, *Recollections of the War of 1812* — a primary source in his own hand. **[DCB: Crooks][Recollections]**

During the **American occupation of Niagara (May–December 1813)**, invading forces **destroyed Crookston** along with much of the town — the same bombardment, opening on **27 May 1813**, that razed the estate. James Crooks reckoned his wartime losses at **more than £9,700**; he would recover only about **£4,450**, and "never fully recovered from these severe financial setbacks." **[DCB: Crooks]**

## 5. The ship that never came home (8 August 1813)

His schooner never came home either — not to him, and not to anyone. Just after **2 a.m. on 8 August 1813**, a sudden **squall off Fourteen Mile Creek** (east of present-day Hamilton) capsized both the **USS *Scourge*** — Crooks's *Lord Nelson* — and the **USS *Hamilton*.** About **84 men drowned** in minutes. **[USS Scourge — Wikipedia]**

The two schooners still lie on the floor of Lake Ontario, upright and astonishingly intact — the **"Hamilton and Scourge,"** among the **best-preserved wooden warships in the world**, a **National Historic Site of Canada (1976)**, owned by the City of Hamilton and legally protected (as war graves) under the Ontario Heritage Act. The Crooks family's finest ship became, and remains, a monument on the lakebed. **[USS Scourge — Wikipedia]**

## 6. Peace — and a hundred-year grievance (1814–1930)

The **Treaty of Ghent (December 1814)** ended the war and fixed the border that had shaped the Crookses' whole lives. Broken financially, James Crooks left the frontier: by **November 1814** he had resettled at **West Flamborough**, on 400 acres bought from Elizabeth Russell, where he built the mills, store and works that became **Crooks' Hollow** — and, in **1825–26**, with provincial backing, **Upper Canada's first paper mill.** He served in the **House of Assembly (from 1820)** and the **Legislative Council (from 1831)**, and died at West Flamborough on **2 March 1860, aged 81.** **[DCB: Crooks]**

But peace did not settle the *Lord Nelson*. The family's claim against the **United States government** became one of the longest-running in the two nations' history:
- **11 July 1817** — a U.S. court for the **Northern District of New York ruled the seizure illegal.** Yet **no money reached the Crookses** — the **court clerk had embezzled the funds.** **[USS Scourge — Wikipedia]**
- For **another 97 years** the descendants pressed on. With the lawyer **Henry James Bethune**, they finally won an award in **1914** — the original **$5,000 plus nearly a century of interest**, totalling **$23,644.38** (reduced to **$15,546.63** after costs), divided among **25 heirs** of James Crooks. Some sources record the payment reaching the family only in **1930.** **[USS Scourge — Wikipedia][DCB: Crooks]**

More than a **century** after a schooner was taken nine days before a war, a Scottish-Canadian family finally closed its account with the American government.

## 7. War ground becomes common ground (1886)

The estate itself waited. For **seventy years** after the fire, Crooks's heirs held the ruined ground on Mississauga Point. Then, in **1886**, they sold it to the **Niagara Assembly** — and the point that had been **bombarded by Americans in 1813** became a **Chautauqua**, a movement of lectures and music that deliberately **drew Americans back across the lake** as friends. The steamers from **Buffalo and Lewiston**, the excursion trains advertised in the **Buffalo Evening News**, the church picnics from western New York — all of it turned a **site of war into a place of cross-border peace.** The wheel of streets rose on the ashes of Crookston; where a family had been torn apart by a border, a summer city was built to reach across it. **[GAC]** — see [chautauqua.md](chautauqua.md).

## Why this belongs at the front of the story
The neighbourhood's whole arc is really the story of one piece of ground: **war → ruin → a hundred-year grievance → reconciliation → community.** The Crooks roots are not a prologue to be skipped; they are the reason the peace of Chautauqua — and, a century later, its Neighbourhood-of-the-Year sociability — means what it does.

## Things that surprise even locals
- Chautauqua's ground once belonged to a family whose **ship was seized by the U.S. Navy nine days before the War of 1812 was declared** — and who **fought the American government for compensation for a hundred years.** **[USS Scourge]**
- That ship, the **Lord Nelson**, renamed **USS *Scourge***, **still lies intact on the floor of Lake Ontario** — a National Historic Site and war grave. **[USS Scourge]**
- The man who owned the Chautauqua land, **James Crooks**, fought at **Queenston Heights**, wrote his own **memoir of the war**, and went on to build **Upper Canada's first paper mill.** **[DCB: Crooks]**
- The **Americans who once bombarded this point** became, within two generations, its **paying summer guests** — Chautauqua turned a battlefield into a meeting place.
